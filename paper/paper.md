# SERAPH: Scale-Adaptive Entity-Resolved Assembly of Prior-Adapted Hyperbolic Gaussian Fields

## Abstract
Traditional 3D reconstruction from unorganized street level imagery often fails because it lacks any understanding of what it sees and it only tries to minimize pixel errors without knowing if the resulting geometry actually makes sense in the real world. We present SERAPH which is an architecture that treats city scale reconstruction as a Bayesian inference problem by combining a powerful semantic prior with a differentiable 3D Gaussian Splatting likelihood. The system moves away from classical point-based matching by discovering distinct persistent entities using a self-supervised transformer backbone and then organizing them into a hyperbolic scene graph to solve for camera poses. By using prior-adapted Gaussian fields for every entity we can reconstruct complex urban localities from sparse views with high geometric accuracy and because the assembly is handled by a scale-aware transformer the model supports seamless level of detail from aerial to ground level perspectives. This paper explores how the integration of hyperbolic geometry and semantic priors allows for a fundamentally more robust representation of urban environments compared to existing pixel centric optimization methods.

## Introduction: The Locality Reconstruction Problem
If you have a phone or a camera or maybe even a drone and you walk around a neighborhood or a market area you end up collecting a massive pile of unorganized videos and photos that have no clear structure. Some images overlap in ways that make sense and others dont and the camera might be close to a building one second and far away the next so you have no precise GPS and certainly no LiDAR or laser scanner data to help you. The goal is to take this messy pile of raw images and turn them into a complete and photorealistic 3D model that you can navigate through from any angle while also having geometric accuracy. No existing system really does this fully because they all assume they are just working with a cloud of points and they dont understand that a building is a building or that a road is a road.

Existing methods like Block-NeRF or 3D Gaussian Splatting or InfNeRF all share a common flaw because they only optimize for the likelihood of the images given the scene which is just a rendering error check. They dont have any prior knowledge about what the world looks like so they have a zero prior where every single 3D scene is equally probable before they even start looking at the images. This is exactly why they fail with sparse inputs because when you only have ten photos there are a thousand different 3D layouts that explain those photos perfectly well but most of them are total nonsense in reality. SERAPH is built to solve this by explicitly modeling both the likelihood via a renderer and the prior via a set of entity models so it can steer the reconstruction toward something that actually looks like a real locality.

## The Mathematical Framework of SERAPH
To understand how the entire system breathes together you have to start with the Bayesian inference framing where we are looking for the probability of the scene given our images. This probability is proportional to the product of the likelihood which is $P(\text{images} \mid \text{scene})$ and the prior which is $P(\text{scene})$ and this simple equation dictates every architectural decision we made. The likelihood term is handled by our 3D Gaussian Splatting renderer which computes how well a proposed scene explains the observed pixels by rendering them and comparing them to truth. Most existing methods only look at this term which is why they get lost in sparse datasets because they have no anchor to reality.

The $P(\text{scene})$ term is our prior and it encodes everything we know about how buildings are vertical and roads are flat and trees have fractal like messy geometry before we even see our specific locale. Our Global Entity Priors implement this prior term and everything else in the SERAPH architecture is just the machinery required to make optimizing this product tractable at the scale of an entire city district. By explicitly modeling the prior we ensure that the optimization is always biased toward semantically meaningful structures and this enables us to reconstruct parts of the scene that were only seen from one or two angles. It is this combination of a generative prior and a high fidelity likelihood that makes the system work where others fail and it is also what makes it efficient enough to run on consumer hardware.

## Component 1: The Entity Discovery Network
The first question the system asks when it looks at a pile of images is what things actually exist in this scene and this is something that regular photogrammetry never bothers with. Tools like COLMAP treat every pixel as equally important and just try to find matching corners which works for textures but fails completely on smooth walls or the sky or a shiny road. This leaves you with huge holes in your 3D points and because the resulting cloud has no concept of objects you cant even ask the model where a specific building is located. The Entity Discovery Network solves this by identifying semantic entities across all images and linking them together so the system understands that pixels in image three and image forty seven are actually the same building.

We use a DINOv2 ViT-L/14 backbone for this because it was pre-trained using self-distillation on 142 million images and it developed a remarkable property where its patch features are semantically coherent. Even without any labels or supervision the features produced by DINOv2 for a window or a road look very similar regardless of the lighting or the viewpoint which is exactly what we need to find correspondences across unorganized photos. For each input image we extract these 1024 dimensional features for every 14 by 14 patch and then we concatenate all these tokens into one giant sequence that represents the whole scene. This gives us a matrix of tokens where we can apply cross-image attention to let every patch talk to every other patch and find out which ones belong to the same physical object.

Because we have so many tokens when we process a hundred images we use a windowed attention mechanism with learnable routing so that we dont crash the GPU memory while still allowing for long range connections. After several rounds of this attention the tokens that belong to the same entity have very similar representations and so we can group them using a differentiable k-means clustering head. This head uses a soft assignment matrix where every token has a probability of belonging to each of the discovered entities and this gives us the soft visibility masks we need for training. We also have a semantic head that takes the cluster centroids and classifies them into one of six classes like building or road or vegetation and this tells the system which prior it should use for each entity it found.

## Component 2: Hyperbolic Scene Graphs and HGNN
After the entities are discovered we have no idea where the cameras were or where the entities are situated in 3D space and we resolve this using a hyperbolic scene graph. Camera pose estimation is really a geometric consistency problem about how the cameras and entities are observed together and so we encode these constraints into a graph structure. We use hyperbolic geometry specifically the Lorentz model because urban scenes have a natural hierarchy from districts down to windows and hierarchies grow exponentially which fits perfectly in a hyperbolic space. In a flat Euclidean space you need hundreds of dimensions to represent a tree without distortion but in a 2D hyperbolic space it embeds perfectly and this means we can represent the whole locality layout very efficiently.

Every entity and every camera gets a node in this graph and we project their features onto the Lorentz manifold using an exponential map that moves them from a flat tangent space onto the curved hyperboloid. We build edges between cameras and entities based on co-visibility so if a camera saw an entity the nodes are pulled together and we also build hierarchy edges that connect coarser entities to their fine detailed children. We then run a hyperbolic graph neural network that performs message passing using the Einstein midpoint to aggregate neighbor information and this updates the positions of every node. After several rounds of this the layout converges and we can extract explicit camera poses in SE(3) space by passing the hyperbolic camera nodes through a small pose decoder MLP.

## Component 3: Prior-Adapted Gaussian Fields
Now that we have poses and entities we need to build the 3D representation and we use 3D Gaussian Splatting because it is much faster than traditional NeRF volumetric rendering. A 3DGS scene is a set of primitives described by their centers and covariances and opacities and spherical harmonic coefficients for view dependent color but we dont start from scratch like most people do. Standard 3DGS usually starts from a random cloud of points which is slow and messy but SERAPH uses a Global Entity Prior that already knows what a building or a tree looks like. These priors are conditional generative models that take a style code from the entity features and produce a baseline Gaussian field that is already a good approximation of the object.

For each specific entity in your locality we take this prior field and we add a Local Residual Adapter which is a small set of trainable Gaussians that only has to learn the fine details that are unique to this specific building. This LRA is initialized at zero and then it grows and splits as needed during training but it is always regularized so that it stays as a sparse residual to the prior. This structure means that even if you have zero views of an entity's roof the prior will at least provide a plausible roof structure based on thousands of other buildings it saw during pre-training. This prevents the "floater" artifacts and geometric holes that plague other methods when the camera coverage is not perfect and it also lets us train each entity independently in parallel which saves a huge amount of memory.

## Component 4: Global Assembly and Rendering
To render a full novel view of the locality we have to assemble all these independent entity fields back into a single world space and this is handled by the Global Assembly Transformer. When we want to render a pixel we samples points along a query ray and we construct a query vector that includes the 3D position and the ray direction and especially the distance from the camera. We project this query into the same hyperbolic space as our scene graph and we compute attention weights over all the entity nodes so that we find the most relevant entities for that specific point in space. This distance parameter is crucial because it acts as an automatic level of detail switch where distant sample points attend to coarse high level entities and nearby points attend to fine detailed entity adapters.

The GAT then fetches the Gaussians from the top entities and transforms them into world space where they are blended together using the attention weights and then passed to the differentiable renderer. Because the blending is handled by this attention mechanism the boundaries between entities like where a facade meets the pavement are handled implicitly and they look smooth without any visible seams. We render these assembled primitives using the gsplat library and we compare the rendered image to our source photos using a combination of L1 and SSIM losses. The gradients from this loss flow all the way back through the assembly transformer and the Gaussian parameters and even the hyperbolic pose decoder so that every single part of the system is refined end to end to achieve maximum photorealism.

## Conclusion and Implementation Phases
The training of SERAPH happens in three distinct phases starting with the offline pre-training of the priors on large public datasets like Waymo and Cityscapes. Phase 1 is the scene organization where we feed your images and run the Entity Discovery Network to build the scene graph and converge the hyperbolic layout which takes about an hour. Phase 2 is the fine tuning where each GPU in your cluster trains a subset of the entity adapters in parallel and refines the camera poses and the assembly weights which takes a few more hours. Because the architecture is so modular and entity centric it fits perfectly on local consumer hardware like your RTX and Tesla setup while still producing city scale models that are navigatable in real time. SERAPH represents a shift from raw optimization to semantically aware 3D learning and it offers a robust solution for the next generation of photorealistic digital twins.
