# SERAPH: Scale-Adaptive Entity-Resolved Assembly of Prior-Adapted Hyperbolic Gaussian Fields

## Abstract
Traditional 3D reconstruction from unorganized street level imagery often fails because it lacks any understanding of what it sees and it only tries to minimize pixel errors without knowing if the resulting geometry actually makes sense in the real world. We present SERAPH which is an architecture that treats city scale reconstruction as a Bayesian inference problem by combining a powerful semantic prior with a differentiable 3D Gaussian Splatting likelihood. The system moves away from classical point-based matching by discovering distinct persistent entities using a self-supervised transformer backbone and then organizing them into a hyperbolic scene graph to solve for camera poses. By using prior-adapted Gaussian fields for every entity we can reconstruct complex urban localities from sparse views with high geometric accuracy and because the assembly is handled by a scale-aware transformer the model supports seamless level of detail from aerial to ground level perspectives.

## Introduction
Capturing the complexity of a neighborhood or a campus with just a handheld camera is fundamentally difficult because the resulting data is usually just a pile of unorganized photos with no precise GPS or LiDAR depth information. Most current systems like Block-NeRF or standard 3D Gaussian Splatting only care about how well the 3D model explains the images and they have absolutely zero knowledge about what a building or a road should look like before the optimization starts. This lack of a prior means that when you only have a few images from sparse angles the optimization just falls into a hole of infinite possible configurations that all look the same to the pixel loss but are geometrically garbage. SERAPH changes this by explicitly building a world prior into the reconstruction loop so that the model already knows the general characteristics of urban objects before it even looks at your specific photos.

The core of our approach is the realization that a city is not just a cloud of points but a collection of discrete entities that relate to each other in a hierarchical way and so we build the entire system around this entity centric view. We use a transformer to find these entities across all images simultaneously and then we place them into a hyperbolic space because that is where hierarchies naturally live without getting distorted. This allows us to estimate camera poses and scene structure through geometric consistency alone and it replaces the need for hours of pre-processing with classical tools like COLMAP. Every building and every road becomes its own adaptive Gaussian field that starts from a general prior so the training is much faster and more robust even on hardware that doesn't have hundreds of gigabytes of memory.

## Philosophical and Mathematical Framing
To really understand why SERAPH is built this way you have to look at the Bayesian perspective where we are looking for the most probable 3D scene given our evidence which is the collection of images we took. If we say that the probability of a scene is proportional to the likelihood of the images given that scene multiplied by the prior probability of the scene itself then we can see where everyone else is going wrong. They only focus on the likelihood and they completely ignore the prior which basically means they think every single possible 3D arrangement is equally likely to exist in the world but we know that isn't true because buildings are usually vertical and roads are flat. SERAPH is the first system that actually puts a number on that prior by training global models of what different classes of objects look like and so the optimization is always being pulled toward a realistic result.

## Entity Discovery and Backbone Features
A human looking at a street doesn't see raw pixels and instead they immediately see buildings and cars and the sky so the first thing SERAPH does is run an Entity Discovery Network to find these things. We use DINOv2 as our backbone because it was trained on millions of images without any labels and it learned that patches belonging to the same kind of object should have very similar feature vectors regardless of the lighting or the angle. Because these features are so semantically consistent we can stack a cross-image attention layer on top and let every patch across every image talk to each other to find out if they belong to the same building even if those images don't have much geometric overlap. We group these tokens using a differentiable k-means approach and that gives us soft masks for every entity which tells us which pixels belong to which object in every single photo and it also tells us if it is a building or a road via a small semantic head.

## Hyperbolic Scene Graphs and Pose Estimation
Once we know what entities exist we have to figure out where they are and where the camera was and this is usually a huge bottleneck but we solve it by building a scene graph in hyperbolic space using the Lorentz model. We use hyperbolic geometry because urban scenes are naturally hierarchical like a tree and trees grow exponentially so they fit perfectly into a space that also grows exponentially at its boundaries. Every camera and every entity gets a position on this hyperboloid and we run a hyperbolic graph neural network to update these positions based on who sees who in the images. If two cameras saw the same building then they are pulled together and if they saw different things they are pushed apart and because this whole process is differentiable we can solve for camera poses through pure geometric consistency. This gives us a layout where the spatial relationships are already established before we even start the fine-grained 3D reconstruction and it makes the whole system handle both close-up and far-away views without any manual tuning.

## Prior-Adapted Gaussian Fields
Representing the actual 3D shape and appearance is done using 3D Gaussian Splatting which is a collection of 3D blobs that can be rendered very fast but we don't start them from scratch. Instead we use a Global Entity Prior for each class which is a pre-trained model that already knows the general "style" of a building or a tree and can produce a baseline 3D field from just a feature vector. For your specific scene we take this baseline and we add a Local Residual Adapter which is a tiny set of trainable Gaussians that only has to learn the specific details and textures that the prior missed. This means the optimization doesn't have to figure out the basics of a building's shape every time and instead it just focuses on the unique features of your locality which makes the training much more efficient and much higher quality for sparse datasets.

## Global Assembly and View Synthesis
The final step is putting all these independent entity fields back together to render a full image and for this we use a Global Assembly Transformer that uses hyperbolic attention. When you want to render a pixel we shoot a ray and we sample points along it and for each point we ask the hyperbolic scene graph which entities are the most relevant based on the distance and the scale. If the camera is far away the attention weights pull from coarse high-level entities but if the camera is close it pulls from the fine details of specific building adapters. This assembly happens on the fly and it blends the entity boundaries together smoothly so you never see any seams where a building meets the road. We render these assembled Gaussians using a differentiable rasterizer and we compare the result to your original images to get the final gradients that refine everything from the 3D shapes all the way back to the camera poses.

## Conclusion
SERAPH provides a new way to think about large scale 3D reconstruction by moving the complexity into the architectural priors and the semantic understanding of the scene. By combining self-supervised vision features with hyperbolic graph reasoning and prior-adapted Gaussian fields we can turn a pile of unorganized images into a photorealistic and navigable locality model. This approach is not only more robust to sparse inputs but it is also much more memory efficient because it breaks the world into entities that can be handled independently and then assembled intelligently. We believe that this entity-centric and prior-driven architecture is the right path forward for creating the next generation of digital twins for entire cities and localities using nothing but raw consumer imagery.
