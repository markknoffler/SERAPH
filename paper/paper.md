# SERAPH: Scale-Adaptive Entity-Resolved Assembly of Prior-Adapted Hyperbolic Gaussian Fields

## Abstract
Traditional 3D reconstruction from unorganized street level imagery often fails because it lacks any understanding of what it sees and it only tries to minimize pixel errors without knowing if the resulting geometry actually makes sense in the real world. We present SERAPH which is an architecture that treats city scale reconstruction as a Bayesian inference problem by combining a powerful semantic prior with a differentiable 3D Gaussian Splatting likelihood. The system moves away from classical point-based matching by discovering distinct persistent entities using a self-supervised transformer backbone and then organizing them into a hyperbolic scene graph to solve for camera poses. By using prior-adapted Gaussian fields for every entity we can reconstruct complex urban localities from sparse views with high geometric accuracy and because the assembly is handled by a scale-aware transformer the model supports seamless level of detail from aerial to ground level perspectives. This paper explores how the integration of hyperbolic geometry and semantic priors allows for a fundamentally more robust representation of urban environments compared to existing pixel centric optimization methods.

## Introduction
The recent progress in neural radiance fields and 3D Gaussian splatting has significantly advanced the field of photo-realistic novel-view synthesis but most of these advancements have been confined to single objects or limited-scale scenes. Large-scale scene reconstruction has a wide range of applications such as land surveys and search and rescue operations but the challenge of effectively representing extensive urban localities remains largely unresolved. Most existing large-scale NeRF approaches like Block-NeRF and Mega-NeRF divide the whole radiance field equally in space into smaller manageable blocks but this grid partition proves insufficient in scenarios requiring a bird-eye view or when handling sparse unorganized inputs. These methods are prone to aliasing artifacts that arise from an insufficient sampling rate for high-frequency signals and they usually lack any hierarchical understanding of the scene geometry.

SERAPH is designed to address these challenges by introducing a scale-adaptive entity-resolved framework that leverages hierarchical priors. You have a phone or a camera or maybe a drone and you walk around a locality and you end up collecting a massive pile of unobserved photos that have no clear structure. Some images overlap in ways that make sense and others dont and the camera might be close to a building one second and far away the next so you have no precise GPS and certainly no LiDAR or laser scanner data to help you. The goal of SERAPH is to take this messy pile of raw images and turn them into a complete and photorealistic 3D model that you can navigate through from any angle while also having geometric accuracy. No existing system really does this fully because they all assume they are just working with a cloud of points and they dont understand that a building is a building or that a road is a road.

## The Mathematical Framework: Bayesian Scene Inference
Before touching any component of the architecture we must understand the unifying mathematical framing because every decision flows from it. Bayesian inference says that given evidence our images what is the most probable explanation the 3D scene and this is expressed as $P(\text{scene} \mid \text{images}) \propto P(\text{images} \mid \text{scene}) \cdot P(\text{scene})$. The first term $P(\text{images} \mid \text{scene})$ is the likelihood which tells us how well a proposed 3D scene explains our observed images and this is what every existing method like Block-NeRF or 3DGS optimizes. They only optimize this term and they have absolutely zero knowledge about what a building or a road should look like before the optimization starts which is why they fail with sparse inputs.

The second term $P(\text{scene})$ is the prior and it encodes our knowledge about what the world looks like before we even look at any images. Buildings are vertical rectangular structures and roads are flat textured planes and trees have irregular fractal-like geometry but no existing method models this term at all. SERAPH is the first architecture that explicitly models both terms where the Global Entity Priors implement the prior and the 3DGS renderer implements the likelihood. Everything else in the architecture is machinery that makes computing and optimizing this product tractable at locality scale and by explicitly modeling the prior we ensure that the optimization is always biased toward semantically meaningful structures.

## Entity Discovery Network: Discovering Semantics from Raw Pixels
The very first question when you look at a pile of unorganized images is what things actually exist in this scene and classical photogrammetry never asks this question. COLMAP treats every pixel as equally important and just tries to find matching corners across images but this creates massive problems for textureless surfaces like smooth walls or roads because they have no corners. If two images dont geometrically overlap enough COLMAP cannot match them even if they contain the same building and the resulting point cloud has no semantic meaning. The Entity Discovery Network solves all three of these problems by answering what are the semantically distinct persistent entities in this scene and which pixels in which images correspond to each entity.

The backbone of the EDN is DINOv2 ViT-L/14 which is a Vision Transformer pre-trained using self-supervised learning on a carefully curated dataset of 142 million images. The remarkable property that emerges from this training is that patch features become semantically coherent so if you take any image and extract the patch features patches that belong to similar things like windows or facades will have very similar feature vectors. This is view-invariant enough that patches showing a building facade from different angles will have similar feature vectors and the cross-image attention will discover the correspondence. For an input image of resolution $H \times W$ DINOv2 divides it into a grid of patches and each patch produces a 1024-dimensional feature vector and these weights are completely frozen during all phases of SERAPH.

Standard transformers apply attention within one image but what we need is attention across all images simultaneously so we concatenate all tokens from all images into one giant sequence. We apply standard multi-head self-attention over this full sequence $\mathbf{T} = [\mathbf{F}_1 \| \mathbf{F}_2 \| \cdots \| \mathbf{F}_K]$ where each token can attend to every other token across all images. During training patch tokens showing semantically similar features will attend to each other strongly even if they were taken from very different angles. The cross-image attention layers are learnable and are stacked on top of DINOv2 and we use windowed attention with learnable routing to handle the massive number of tokens produced by a locality-scale dataset. The clustering head then groups these tokens into entity clusters using differentiable k-means which casts cluster assignment as a soft attention problem.

## Hyperbolic Scene Graphs and Geometric Consistency
After the EDN runs we have a set of entities but we still have unknown camera poses and no idea where any camera was when it took its photo. Every existing method solves this with COLMAP which is not differentiable and takes hours on large scenes but SERAPH replaces it entirely with a differentiable neural component. The key insight is that camera pose estimation is a geometric consistency problem not a feature matching problem so we need to find camera positions such that the co-visibility relationships are geometrically consistent. The hyperbolic scene graph encodes these constraints as a graph and the HGNN solves for the camera poses by finding the hyperbolic embedding that satisfies all constraints simultaneously.

The scene has a hierarchy from sky to districts and streets down to facades and windows and this hierarchy is a tree that grows exponentially. Euclidean space grows polynomially so representing this hierarchy in flat space requires enormous dimensionality and significant distortion but in 2D hyperbolic space the same tree embeds perfectly with near-zero distortion. We use the Lorentz model for our hyperbolic representation because it is numerically stable during backpropagation compared to the Poincaré disk. The Lorentz model is the surface $\mathcal{H}^n = \{ \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n+1} : -x_0^2 + x_1^2 + \cdots + x_n^2 = -1, x_0 > 0 \}$ and every entity and every camera gets a point on this surface.

Entity nodes are created by applying a projection MLP to the DINOv2 features and then applying the exponential map at the origin to reach the hyperboloid surface. Camera nodes are created similarly from mean-pooled patch tokens and both live on the same hyperbolic manifold so the geometry of the graph immediately encodes the spatial relationships between them. We build co-visibility edges between entities and observation edges between cameras and entities and hierarchy edges that form the structural backbone of the locality. The HGNN runs message passing by aggregating neighbor messages using the Einstein midpoint which is the mathematically correct weighting for Lorentzian geometry. We transform these messages in the tangent space and then push them back onto the manifold and the whole system is optimized via a layout loss that pulls connected nodes together and pushes unrelated nodes apart.

## Prior-Adapted Gaussian Fields for Entity Representation
Once we have our scene organized we need to built an explicit 3D representation of each entity that can be rendered from any viewpoint. We use 3D Gaussian Splatting for this but standard 3DGS has critical problems for city-scale scenes because it starts from scratch and trains all entities simultaneously. The Per-Entity Prior-Adapted Gaussian Fields solve both by pre-training a prior that already knows what entities look like and using it to initialize tiny per-entity adapters that are trained independently. A 3DGS scene is a set of Gaussian primitives characterized by a center covariance opacity and spherical harmonic coefficients for view-dependent color. Rendering is achieved by projecting these Gaussians onto the image plane and alpha-compositing them front to back which is fast and differentiable.

We build a Global Entity Prior for each semantic class which is a conditional generative model trained on large public datasets and this MLP takes a canonical 3D position and a style code to output the Gaussian parameters. At inference for a new building we run an encoder to map the DINOv2 feature to a style code that captures the specific character of that building. Each discovered entity then gets this prior Gaussian field and a trainable Local Residual Adapter which is a small 3DGS field initialized at zero. The full representation is the sum of the prior and the adapter where the prior provides the baseline vertical structure and the adapter learns fine-grained textures and specific geometric deviations. This structure means the optimization only has to fit a small residual which makes it much faster and much more robust for sparse viewed entities.

## Global Assembly and Scale-Adaptive View Synthesis
To render a novel view of the entire locality we need to transform every entity field into world space and determine which entities are relevant for any given viewpoint. The Global Assembly Transformer handles this by using a cross-entity attention mechanism where we sample points along a query ray and encode their position and ray direction into a query vector. We also include the camera-to-point distance in this query which is the crucial mechanism for automatic level of detail. A large distance means the camera is far from the point so the query attends to coarse high-level entities but a small distance means the camera is close and the query attends to fine-detail leaf entities.

The GAT computes attention weights over all entity nodes in the hyperbolic scene graph and fetch Gaussians only from the most relevant entities for each ray sample. These blended density and color values are then passed to the standard 3DGS rasterizer and the boundary between entities like where a facade meets the road is handled implicitly by the attentionweighted sum. This architecture ensures that we only query the entities visible from the current viewpoint at the appropriate scale which makes the system extremely memory efficient. We render these assembled Gaussians using CUDA-accelerated differentiable rasterizers and we compare the results to real pixels using a perception-aware loss that combines L1 difference and the Structural Similarity Index.

## Differentiable Rendering and Optimization Loop
The final step is the end-to-end optimization where gradients from the photometric loss flow back through the entire chain from the compositor to the camera poses. We use the gsplat library for fast differentiable rasterization and we compare rendered pixels to real pixels to calculate the final error. The gradients tell the system how to adjust Gaussian positions and opacities and colors and even how to refine the camera poses via the pose decoder in the HGNN. Training happens in three phases including GEP pre-training on public data and scene organization to converge the hyperbolic layout and then per-entity adapter fine-tuning to refine the locality details. This entity-centric and prior-driven architecture is feasible on consumer hardware configurations while being impossible for existing methods at comparable scene scale and we believe this represents a foundation for scalable and efficient large-scale scene representation.
